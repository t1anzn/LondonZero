# City of London — Junction Design & Vision Zero (mock excerpt)

Source: City of London Corporation Transport Strategy and "All Change at Bank" scheme (illustrative summary for LondonZero demo).

## Complex multi-arm junctions
- Junctions with five or more arms (e.g. Bank Junction) carry elevated collision risk due to multiple conflicting movements and driver decision load.
- Simplification — reducing the number of permitted movements, banning through-traffic, or restricting motor vehicle access — is the preferred treatment for the most complex, high-casualty junctions.
- The "All Change at Bank" scheme restricted Bank Junction to buses and cycles only between 7am–7pm on weekdays, reducing casualties significantly.

## Movement separation and legibility
- Separate conflicting movements in time (signal phasing) or space (dedicated lanes) at high-complexity junctions.
- Improve legibility: clear lane markings, simplified geometry, and reduced carriageway width to lower vehicle speeds.

## Regulatory and feasibility constraints
- Heritage and listed-building context in the City constrains kerb realignment and signal pole placement.
- Bus route continuity and emergency vehicle access must be maintained in any restriction scheme.
- Loading and servicing access for adjacent premises must be considered, typically via time-restricted windows.

## Recommended treatment hierarchy for high-casualty City junctions
1. Restrict/remove through motor traffic where feasible.
2. Separate vulnerable road users from remaining traffic.
3. Simplify geometry and improve crossings and sightlines.
